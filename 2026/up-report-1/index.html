About the report

⚠ This report is intended for internal team use only. The source data contains phone numbers — please be mindful before sharing the source file externally.

1
How messages were grouped. Each person's messages sent within 5 minutes of each other were treated as a single exchange. This reduced the raw rows to 37 grouped exchanges. One was identified as spam and excluded, leaving 36 clusters through April 2026. A June 2026 refresh added 7 further exchanges, for 43 in total, which form the basis of all figures in this report.
2
Messages were in Hindi. Unlike the Karnataka group, UP messages were not provided with an English translation — analysis was conducted directly on the original Hindi text. Quoted content in this report is translated for readability.
3
Photos and media are read in context. Media files cannot be opened from a WhatsApp export. Where a photo was sent alongside a caption or text in the same exchange, the text was used to understand what the media was about. Photos sent with no text at all are noted but not categorised.

Key themes

Key themes

Based on councillor messages only

CLDP Programme Engagement

The dominant theme across the full period. In October, councillors immediately flagged the broken photo link and asked for workshop materials (budget list, scheme list) — showing intent to engage but a blocked path. In March, the group came alive around the Delhi Leadership Conference: five councillors wrote substantive thank-you messages, three of which were detailed testimonials about the programme's impact on their working style.

Greetings & Celebrations

New Year wishes from three councillors on 1 January; Republic Day media and greetings on 26 January; Holi wishes from two councillors in early March. Over a third of all councillor clusters fall in this category. The group is active during festivals even when quiet otherwise — suggesting these moments could be used as re-engagement opportunities.

Community / General

One councillor (added in March) shared a YouTube video and media in late March and April. The content could not be fully verified from the export but appears to be locally relevant. No field updates — road work, health camps, or ward activities — were shared, in contrast to the Karnataka group. June 2026 brought a notable first: councillor Tarik Choudhary opened a substantive governance discussion on holding monthly board meetings under Section 86 of the Municipality Act 1916, drawing replies from Shabana Tarik and Mohd Yunus — the group's first genuine councillor-to-councillor policy exchange.

Month-on-month

Month-on-month progress

Message clusters by month, split by sender type

Despite two months of complete silence after launch, the group's overall trajectory is upward. March 2026 (13 clusters) exceeded the launch month (Oct 2025, 7 clusters) and was the most active period in the group's history — driven by the Delhi conference and poll engagement. The pattern suggests the group responds to event-linked prompts but goes dormant without them. After a silent May, June 2026 brought the group's first councillor-led policy discussion (monthly board meetings) and a fourth poll — a small but qualitatively important step beyond greetings and thank-yous.

Oct 2025

The only month with substantive two-way dialogue. Councillors immediately flagged the broken photo link and requested workshop materials — showing real intent to engage, blocked by a technical issue that was never fully resolved. The budget and scheme lists they requested were not shared in the group.

Nov – Dec 2025

Complete silence for two consecutive months. The group went dormant within six weeks of launch. No Janagraha posts, no councillor messages. This is the starkest drop of any state group in the dataset and likely reflects a lack of structured follow-up after the October workshop.

Jan – Feb 2026

January's revival was entirely social — greetings with no substantive exchange. February saw the highest admin output of any single month (5 Janagraha clusters) but drew zero councillor responses. Document drops and news articles did not translate to engagement.

March 2026

Best month by volume and quality. The CLDP Delhi Leadership Conference generated the most substantive councillor content of the entire period — five written responses including three detailed testimonials praising the programme in Hindi. The term number poll also achieved the group's highest participation rate (33%). Both confirm that event-linked, personal prompts work; document drops do not.

May – Jun 2026

May was silent. June marked a turn in quality: councillor Tarik Choudhary initiated a genuine governance discussion on monthly board meetings under the Municipality Act, and two other councillors replied — the first peer-to-peer policy exchange in the group's history. The June quiz drew only 4 votes, and notably the most-voted answer was incorrect, flagging a clear follow-up training opportunity.
